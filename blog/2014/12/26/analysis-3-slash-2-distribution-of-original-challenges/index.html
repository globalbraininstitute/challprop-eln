Processes per agent

When an agent is challenged with the situation by generator of diversity, the agent may carry on few processes with it, which together form a ‘pipeline’ of processing that challenge. These ‘pipelines’ may differ from each other and are not predictable because the way the agent treats the challenge depends on, e.g. the intensity of it; there is also stochastic component. The usual pipeline of the challenge looks like this:

==>e[1bgbR-8NGA-1w][2097620-challenged->8331964]:
an agent No. 8331964 is challenged by G.O.D by a situation
==>e[1INbz-yXww-1M][8331964-interpreted->2097620]: 
the challenge is interpreted, intensity+ penalty calculated, etc.
==>e[1IN8p-yXww-1o][8331964-buffered->2097620]: 
the challenge is put into queue of an agent;
==>e[1INgV-yXww-22][8331964-processed->2097620]: 
it is processed (extraction of benefit, etc.);
==>e[1INef-yXww-1U][8331964-reinterpreted->2097620]: 
turned back to the situation and prepared for propagation;
==>e[1bgbP-8NGA-1w][2097620-challenged->5122808]: 
propagated further..
==>e[1cPSX-ad28-1E][2433384-forgot->2099552]: 
'sent to sink' so to speak, i.e. the situation drops out of propatation.

We can calculate how many these ‘elementary events’ happened on average for each agent during the simulation time (for the total number of the elementary events, see Analysis 3/1):

Distribution of original challenges

The simulation goes this way: there are 10 thousand agents, 100 iterations. Each iteration 20% of agents get a fresh challenge generated by G-O-D, which is 2000 per iteration. These challenges in the beginning are (should be) distributed randomly across all agents in the network. If challenges are not distributed evenly across the network, that may cause buffers of agents lock some challenges and that would explain why we see somewhat low number of secondary propagation of challenges during further simulation.
